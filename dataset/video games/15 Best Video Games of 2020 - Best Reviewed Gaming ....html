The 15 Best Video Games of 2020 (So Far)

From Final Fantasy VII Remake to Ghost of Tsushima, these are the titles we've already dedicated hours to playing this year.

By Brady Langmann and Cameron Sherrill
Jul 24, 2020
Courtesy

Last year may not have gone down as one of the best years in gaming, but hey, at least we got Untitled Goose Game. The 2010s, however, represented a step forward for the industry, with titles like Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild and the Batman: Arkham series. Now, we’re midway into the first year of a new decade, and in a twilight year too. The console market is quickly shifting. Soon the PS4 and Xbox One will be booted to the trash heap, and we’ll wish them farewell while welcoming in a super-powered generation of machines. The Xbox Series X is bringing heavy-duty tech that rivals the guts of even the strongest PC towers, and the PS5 seems just as formidable.

Related Stories

All the PS5 Games That'll Confirm You Want a PS5

But fret not, the old consoles are going out with a bang in terms of new games. This year has been stacked with titles spanning all audiences, from plenty of action RPGs and first-person shooters, to some insanely narrative-driven games, to some equally artful and wacky indie projects. A new Animal Crossing (!!) and a sequel to The Last of Us have proven that you can always rely on impressive triple-A outtings. Same goes for the always-innovative indie community, with a gem like Moving Out blessing our consoles.

The previous-gen consoles' goodbye tour may be bittersweet, but with boundary-pushing titles like Dreams and Ghost of Tsushima, it has already been a great year. These are the 15 best video games that have been released in 2020 so far. (But if you’re still unhappy with this gaming slate, don't forget that we’ll still be blessed by the divine god SkateBIRD in due time.)

The Last of Us Part II

Courtesy
The Last of Us Part II
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: June 19
Platform: PS4

You have to give Naughty Dog credit: Its team had a nearly impossible chance of following up The Last of Us, which was one of the most beloved games of the last decade. Not to mention, a beginning-to-end journey that was so satisfying that its fans were all but sure a sequel would ruin everything. Turns out, all those delays were well worth it: The Last of Us Part II is, at the very least, on par with the legend it followed, continuing Ellie’s story with the kind of unforgettable heart and heartbreak that we rarely see in video games. —B.L.

Final Fantasy VII Remake

Courtesy
Final Fantasy VII: Remake
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: April 10
Platform: PS4

This year we finally got to return to Midgar, and it was glorious. Sure, any game surrounded by this much hype was bound to have criticism heaped on it, but the remake is a masterful re-imagining of the title that redefined what an RPG could be. Final Fantasy VII Remake introduced an engaging style of action RPG, both melding the party balancing system from traditional RPGs with more fluid, flashy combat, and overturning the turned-based system. By no means does the FF7 remake replace the original. It instead does something even better: It creates what feels like a truly new experience. —C.S.

Animal Crossing: New Horizons

Courtesy
Animal Crossing: New Horizons
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: March 20
Platform: Nintendo Switch

What’s left to say about New Horizons? By now, you’ve likely settled into your forever life with your critter villagers, all of whom you’ve surely spent months cycling in and out so you could share an island with your favorites. So, grab an orange juice, sit next to Henry the frog by the lake you just cleared, and relax. Nice. Best game? Yeah, best game. —B.L.

Ghost of Tsushima

Courtesy
Ghost of Tsushima
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: July 17
Platform: PS4

Easily a contender for my Game of the Year (at least until Fall Guys comes out in a few weeks), Ghost of Tsushima wasn't on my radar nearly as enough as it should have been before its launch (my bad), and now it's all I can play. It is a blend of stunning and non-exhausting open-world adventuring, intensely thoughtful combat, gorgeous landscapes, and in-depth story, peppered with homages to classic samurai cinema. The game is a perfect send-off for the PS4, really showing what this generation ultimately became by taking the tech to its absolute limit. —C.S.

Streets of Rage 4

Courtesy
Streets of Rage 4
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: April 30
Platforms: PS4, Xbox One, PC, Nintendo Switch

This is how a legend returns in style. Streets of Rage 4 is an honor to the classic beat ‘em up, and a brilliant expansion on a semi-forgotten genre. So far I’ve played it through about five times, and it's still not getting old. The combat is stylish and addictive, and the art style is drop dead gorgeous—everything a Sega fanboy could hope for. We’ve seen a lot of older titles get rebooted, especially by the killer indie studio DotEmu (which did Wonder Boy, Windjammers, and more). Streets of Rage 4 is a great game that continues in the spirit of the originals, setting a precedent for more forgotten retro titles to be revived in the future. —C.S.

Moving Out

Courtesy
Moving Out
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: April 28
Platforms: PS4, Xbox One, PC, Mac, Nintendo Switch

Team 17’s at it again with frantic, rage-inducing couch co-op. The folks who brought us Overcooked made the move into the home market with a game that is all about working with a team to pivot, angle, and position furniture. It is a blast. The concept may be simple, but rest assured it will have you feeling all sorts of fury at the execution. Couch co-op itself is often a forgotten form in gaming, but a personal favorite of mine. Nothing like having a few drinks and trying to coordinate with some friends. —C.S.

Murder By Numbers

Courtesy
Murder by Numbers
nintendo.com
Order

Release Date: March 5
Platforms: PC, Nintendo Switch

If you haven’t heard of Murder by Numbers, the best way I can put it is this: Ace Attorney but better. It’s a perfectly handled detective flick with linear problem-solving and logic puzzles. It’s also a great change of pace that’s easily accessible for anyone who loves a good puzzle, a fun story, and well-written dialog. Murder by Numbers stands apart as an immensely inventive and creative game in an industry that tends to favors doing, not thinking. —C.S.

Doom Eternal

Courtesy

Doom Eternal
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: March 20
Platform: PS4, Xbox One, PC, Google Stadia, Nintendo Switch

Speaking of “doing, not thinking,” Doom Eternal is on the flip side. Do hack, slash, chainsaw, and glory kill demons on your way through literal hell. This game is fast-paced, flashy, and everything that made those narc moms on ‘90s TV say, “Video games will rot your brain.” And it's amazing for it. Doom Eternal feels like what we hope a modern TimeSplitters would be, but so polished. It's amazing how far Doom as a series has been pushed since 1993. Folks, there's not a lot more to say here besides: Killing demons has never felt so damn good. —C.S.

Dreams

Courtesy
Dreams
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: February 14
Platform: PS4

Ah, Dreams. Why did you do it? If you haven’t heard, Dreams gives its players nearly unprecedented tools to create their own games, which other users can try out once they’re finished. Yeah. So queue up Dreams and you’ll find… “Wario Goes to McDonald’s.” Not your jam? There’s “Wario Gets Run Over By a Car.” In all fairness, we have seen some truly incredible games in Dreams (check out Sinfeld Chronicles). But really, most people are there for the memes. Aren’t we all? —B.L.

Ori and the Will of Wisps

Courtesy
Ori and the Will of the Wisps
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: March 11
Platforms: Xbox One, PC

Ori and the Will of the Wisps is one of the few 2020 Xbox exclusives out there, and it almost makes up for the lack of others. The Ori series kicked off back in 2015 with Ori and the Blind Forest, which in its own right was a beautiful platform adventure that yanked on your heartstrings through simplistic storytelling and world building. For Will of the Wisps, take everything Blind Forest had going for it and multiply it by ten. It is a mesmerizing experience, featuring one of the best art styles and soundtracks of any game on this list. —C.S.

Half Life: Alyx

Courtesy
Half-Life: Alyx
steampowered.com
Order

Release Date: March 23
Platform: PC

A survival game? Not for the faint of heart. A survival game in virtual reality? Shit. Half Life: Alyx, which is set between Half Life 1 and Half Life 2, is really a see-it-to-believe-it thing. But trust us, it’s the closest experience to our inevitable Ready Player One, all-VR-everything lives we’ve ever had. So, go ahead: Chuck a grenade, watch it go boom, and try to avoid the nightmare monsters hurtling toward your face. —B.L.

Journey to the Savage Planet

Courtesy

Journey to the Savage Planet
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: January 28
Platforms: PS4, Xbox One, PC, Nintendo Switch

Take No Man’s Sky, remove the controversy, add a layer of consistently hilarious dialogue, and you get Journey to the Savage Planet. It’s a game about exploration, which sounds like an early 2011 tagline, but in this case it's actually successful. I found myself playing co-op with my friends for hours. The game is for a pretty specific type of gamer, but that's okay. If you're looking for a title that's not going to force objectives down your throat and instead really let you do it your way, it's Journey to the Savage Planet. If you're just keen on having a good time, look up some of its cutscenes and dialogue, because those alone are more than worth it. —C.S.

Paper Mario: The Origami King

Courtesy
Paper Mario: The Origami King
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: July 17
Platform: Nintendo Switch

I’m a sucker for Paper Mario. People actually have started calling me Paper Cam, probably because I carry a giant hammer around, am paper thin, and always move in the style of turn-based combat. Paper Mario: The Origami King is a wonderfully inventive return to the Paper Mario style we all love. After a series of missteps (looking at you, Sticker Star), the new mechanics and simplified tone feel like an homage to the flat glory days of the N64 and GameCube era. This game is comfort food through and through, and that's something we all could use right now. —C.S.

Clubhouse Games: 51 Worldwide Classics

Courtesy
Clubhouse Games: 51 Worldwide Classics
amazon.com
Order

Release Date: June 5
Platform: Nintendo Switch

Clubhouse-freaking-Games. It’s got everything: chess, blackjack, president, poker, checkers, Connect 4, mancala—a full collection of, you guessed it, 51 games. After getting absolutely thrashed by my editor in mancala, I had picked up Clubhouse to play, learn, and plan for my next strike. Honestly, it is just really well-polished and an all-around good time, especially if you want something a little slower and more accessible. My pandemic goal is to become a full master of all 51 games, and even the bonus piano. —C.S.

Carrion

Courtesy
Carrion
Order

Release Date: July 23
Platforms: Xbox One, PC, Nintendo Switch

Big alien monster. Gore. Space. 16 bit. Metroidvania. If that's not enough to get you up and buying this game immediately, then I don't know how to help you. Carrion has players take control of a menacing space creature in a 16-bit space station, getting them to cleverly strike against the colony inhabitants, and bringing a whole new playstyle and moveset to an otherwise tired gaming genre. The title just came out, but for a $20 price tag it's an easy pick-up-and-go option. Stay scared and carrion, my friends. —C.S.
